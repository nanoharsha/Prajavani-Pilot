Why Change Was Needed Adilabad Prajavani
The Problem

Why the existing system needed reform

Existing grievance redress systems — CM Prajavani, CPGRAMS, District portals — share structural limitations that undermine access and accountability for citizens in rural Telangana.

Limitation
Centralised filing only
Citizens must travel to Hyderabad — hundreds of kilometres — to register a grievance at CM Prajavani.
Limitation
No acknowledgement receipt
No dated proof of filing. Citizens have no record that their complaint was received.
Limitation
No status tracking
Once filed, there is no way to check what happened. Officers face no accountability for delays.
Limitation
No public hearings
Grievances are processed invisibly, without any collective accountability forum.
Limitation
Self-reported resolution
CM Prajavani reports 74% resolved — without independent verification, disposal is mistaken for resolution.
Limitation
No appeal mechanism
If an ATR is unsatisfactory or delayed, there is no formal pathway for citizens to escalate.

The Pilot Journey Adilabad Prajavani
The Journey

From consultation to institutionalisation

The pilot moved from civil society advocacy to a formally institutionalised district-level system within 15 months.

Sep 2024
Civil society public meeting on GRM reform. Consultations with communities across Adilabad.
Jan 2025
Adilabad Pilot formally launched (27 Jan 2025). IFCs activated across 16 mandals. GROs trained on 17 SOPs.
Feb 2025
First round of public hearings — 16 hearings across 16 mandals within 2 weeks. 55%+ complainant attendance.
Nov 2025
Weekly Mandal Prajavani institutionalised — every Monday. Regular hearing rhythm established.
Jan 2026
Mandal Prajavani formally launched with full features: digital portal, ATR tracking, Collector appeal.
Apr 2026
12,741 grievances registered. 82 public hearings held. 7,998 ATRs filed. Model under policy review.

How the Model Works Adilabad Prajavani
The Model

Seven interlocking components

Seven components form a complete grievance redress cycle — from filing at the village level to written, verified resolution.

Village
→
IFC
→
GRO
→
Public Hearing
→
ATR
→
Appeal
IFC
Information Facilitation Centre
Mandal-level centres with trained operators who register grievances and issue dated acknowledgement receipts. Brings filing to the citizen's doorstep — eliminates travel to Hyderabad.
GRO
Grievance Redress Officer
Designated officer responsible for investigating the grievance and filing an ATR within 30 days. Clear ownership, 30-day deadline, and SOPs guide what must be checked.
SOP
Standard Operating Procedures
17 SOPs across 7 departments, covering 20 grievance categories. Each SOP specifies what action the GRO must take. Converts discretion into procedure; makes responses auditable.
PH
Public Hearing
Monthly mandal-level hearings where citizens and GROs meet face to face. SSAAT and civil society facilitate. 82 hearings conducted. GROs must explain their action in public.
ATR
Action Taken Report
A mandatory written response from the GRO within 30 days of filing. Available online and downloadable. Transforms verbal assurances into a tracked, comparable record.
APL
Appeals Process
Citizens unsatisfied with ATR quality or facing delays can escalate to a Collector-level appeal. The Collector personally reviews selected cases. Closes the accountability loop.
DGT
Digital Platform
A dedicated "Adilabad Pilot" tab on prajavani.cgg.gov.in enables 24×7 online grievance filing, OTP-based status tracking, and ATR downloads. Complements the physical IFC channel — digital as enabler, not replacement for the right to be heard.
Core methodological distinction — Disposal ≠ Resolution. An ATR marked "disposed" does not mean the citizen's problem is solved. SSAAT and civil society teams independently verify every closed grievance in the field. The independently verified resolution rate (~52–55%) is materially lower than the system's self-reported disposal rate (74%).

Key Insights & Learnings Adilabad Prajavani
Evidence

Key insights and learnings

What the data and field evidence tell us — including what works, what doesn't, and what is still uncertain.

01
Decentralisation dramatically improves access
Moving filing from Hyderabad to mandal IFCs removed the single largest barrier for rural citizens — the cost and distance of travel. 12,741 grievances in 15 months suggests pent-up demand that existing systems could not capture.
02
Public hearings improve accountability
Mandals with regular public hearings show higher ATR quality and lower rates of "disposal without resolution." Face-to-face accountability creates different incentives than back-office processing.
03
Digital + physical hybrid outperforms either alone
The portal enables 24×7 filing and status tracking, but physical IFCs remain essential for citizens without smartphones or literacy support. Neither channel is sufficient on its own.
04
Independent verification is non-negotiable
The 20+ percentage point gap between system-reported disposal (74%) and field-verified resolution (52–55%) demonstrates that self-reporting cannot substitute for independent accountability.
05
Written SOPs reduce arbitrariness at scale
17 SOPs across 7 departments standardise what GROs must do — and what citizens can expect. Without them, responses vary by officer rather than by merit of the grievance.
Limitations and caveats
Data cut-off
The dataset closes on 31 January 2026. Grievances filed or resolved after this date are not captured. Actual resolution rates are likely higher.
ATR quality variation
The quality of ATR remarks varies significantly across mandals and GROs. Some ATRs consist of a single generic line; others are detailed and verifiable.
Field verification coverage
Not all closed grievances could be verified in person within the reporting period. Coverage is estimated at approximately 70% of closed cases.
Mandal variation
10 of 17 mandals filed zero ATRs in one reporting week despite holding 3,521 pending cases — indicating chronic underperformance in a subset of mandals.

Representative case stories

Six cases — resolved and unresolved — from across the pilot. Names anonymised.

✓ Resolved Revenue · Adilabad (R)
The issue
Land in Prohibited (POB) list for 5+ years. Unable to sell or mortgage. Multiple redirections across departments.
Outcome
Land removed from Prohibited list. Patta issued (April 2026) after Collector personally reviewed case following two delayed ATRs.
"
For five years no one would listen. Within four months of registering at the IFC, I finally have my documents.
— Applicant, Adilabad (R) mandal
○ Unresolved Energy · Boath
The issue
Gruhajyothi (free electricity) not received. Portal shows "Not Applied" but applicant was enrolled in Praja Palana.
Outcome
Pending at State Government — portal correction requires state-level intervention. 79 similar cases in mandal with identical status.
✓ Resolved Panchayat Raj · Utnoor
The issue
MGNREGA wages for 42 days unpaid for 8 months. Bank account linked but transfers failing.
Outcome
Wages released within 19 days. Biometric linking corrected. Public hearing facilitated dialogue with local panchayat officer present.
"
The hearing made the Panchayat officer listen. He could not ignore it in front of everyone.
— Applicant, Utnoor mandal
◑ Partially resolved Panchayat Raj · Bela
The issue
Widow pension stopped for 14 months following biometric failure. GRO ATR notes "permanent migration" — applicant has never left the village.
Outcome
Pension restored for future months after SSAAT field team verified presence. Arrears of 14 months pending state-level policy clarification.
✓ Resolved Forest · Indervelly
The issue
Individual Forest Rights (IFR) title pending for 6 years despite Gram Sabha approval.
Outcome
IFR title issued after sub-divisional review. Two other similar cases in same mandal resolved in same hearing round.
"
The GRO came to our village for the first time in years. The public hearing is what made them move the file.
— Applicant, Indervelly mandal

The issue
Gas subsidy of ₹1,050 (3 cylinders) matched in portal but not disbursed for 8 months.
Outcome
Subsidy release pending state treasury clearance. Confirmed state-level payment gateway issue affecting 79 similar cases in the mandal.
Policy Recommendations Adilabad Prajavani
Way Forward

Policy recommendations

The Adilabad model has demonstrated feasibility. The next challenge is formalisation and replication — in Telangana and beyond.

System integration
High
  • Integrate mandal, district, and state grievance portals into a unified architecture with shared data standards.
  • Establish real-time API linkages between the Adilabad Pilot tab and CM Prajavani backend.
Strengthen appeals
High
  • Formalise the Collector appeal as a statutory right with defined timelines.
  • Extend appeals to a state-level ombudsman for cases involving state-level blocks.
Scale public hearings
High
  • Mandate monthly public hearings at mandal level across all 33 Telangana districts.
  • Include SSAAT and civil society as official facilitators in the Government Order.
Independent verification
Critical
  • Formalise SSAAT's field verification role via Government Order — not just pilot MoU.
  • Publish independently verified resolution rates alongside system-reported disposal rates.
Standardise SOPs statewide
Medium
  • Scale the 17 Adilabad SOPs as a template for all districts in Telangana.
  • Develop a living SOP repository with regular updates as schemes change.
Digital infrastructure
Medium
  • Invest in SMS notifications, multilingual interface, accessibility for feature phone users.
  • Build real-time ATR pendency dashboards for district collectors.
The ask to government
Issue a Government Order recognising SSAAT and civil society as official verification partners. This is the single step that would institutionalise the accountability layer that makes this model work.
The replication opportunity
Multiple states are watching this pilot. Telangana has the opportunity to establish the national standard for decentralised, participatory, verified grievance redress — building on the CM Prajavani brand and infrastructure.
